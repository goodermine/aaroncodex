# VOX — Singer's User Manual

*How to use the software, and what every number and picture in your report
means — in plain language.*

You don't need to read this end to end. Section 3 (the one ruler you must
know) and Section 5 (how to read a report in 30 seconds) are the essentials.
Everything after is a dictionary you can dip into.

---

## 1. What VOX does for you

You give it a recording of you singing. It:
1. Separates your voice from any backing music.
2. Measures your singing with the same tools voice scientists use.
3. Scores you **against real professional recordings** — the people on the
   radio — measured the exact same way.
4. Shows you *where* in the song things went well or wrong, with timestamps.
5. Prescribes one exercise to work on, from a proper vocal-coaching library.
6. Tracks your improvement take after take.

**The promise:** every number is really measured. The system does not guess
your score or flatter you. When it says you're close to the pros, you are.
When it points at a weakness, that weakness is real — and it tells you
exactly what to do about it.

---

## 2. How to use it

**Web app:** open the page, drag in your recording (mp3, m4a, wav…),
optionally type the song and original artist if you want a comparison, and
press analyse. Watch the progress bar (isolating vocals → tracking pitch →
running diagnostics → building report). When it's done you get an
interactive pitch view and a full report.

**Through Candi (chat):** send your recording and say who's singing and what
song. Candi runs the same engine and writes you a coaching reply.

**Tips for a good analysis:**
- A clean, close recording beats a room recording. Less background noise =
  more trustworthy numbers.
- Sing a real stretch of the song (30+ seconds) so there are enough held
  notes to measure.
- If you sang in a different key from the original, that's fine — the
  comparison removes the key difference automatically. A lower key is a
  choice, not an error.

---

## 3. The one ruler you must understand: **cents**

The distance between two neighbouring piano keys is **100 cents**. That's
your unit for everything about pitch.

- Most people can't *hear* a note is off until it's about **10–25 cents** out.
- So "your notes are 20 cents flat" means: slightly under, noticeable to a
  trained ear, very fixable.
- 50 cents = exactly halfway between two notes (as wrong as it gets).

Learn this one thing and half the report reads itself.

## 4. Your scores

**Overall score / 10.** A blend of six measured things (how accurate, how
steady, how clean your tone, vibrato, dynamics, breath). Here's the key:
**a 10 means you matched the middle of the professional pack.** So an 8.8
isn't "B+ homework" — it's "within touching distance of the pros, measured
identically." Nobody is being generous; the pros set the bar.

**"Beats X% of pro references."** Under each part of your score you'll see
this. It's the honest detail. You can score 8.8 overall *and* see
"intonation beats 9% of pros" — both true. The overall tells you how good;
the percentages tell you *which one thing* is holding you back.

**Capture-fair score / 10.** Use this — and only this — when comparing
yourself to the **original recording**. Old records and even great studio
tracks measure "rougher" once they've been through YouTube and voice
separation, through no fault of the singer. The capture-fair score strips
out the parts of the score that unfairly punish the older recording, so a
head-to-head is honest. (It's why the system won't let you "beat" Glenn Frey
on tape hiss.)

**Confidence: high / medium / low.** How much to trust this particular score.
Low confidence usually means a noisy or short recording — treat the numbers
as a guide, not gospel.

**What the score does NOT judge:** your artistry, emotion, or
interpretation. Those are human and precious and deliberately left out of
the number. A great, moving performance and a high technical score are two
different things — you want both, and only one of them is measurable.

---

## 5. How to read a report in 30 seconds

1. Look at the **overall score** — where am I overall?
2. Find the **lowest component** and its **"beats X% of pros"** — that's the
   one thing to fix.
3. Read the **timestamps** for that component — where in the song?
4. Do the **prescribed drill** — the system already picked it for you.

Everything else in the report is the *why* behind that one weakness. You
don't have to read it all to improve.

## 6. What the numbers mean (dictionary)

### About your pitch
- **Median deviation (cents):** where your held notes typically land vs
  dead-centre. Lower is better. ~20 = pro territory; ~10 = excellent.
- **Drift (cents):** whether a note *stays put* after you land it, or slides
  around while you hold it. This is really a **breath-support** number —
  steady air, steady note. Watching this fall over practice sessions is one
  of the most satisfying things in the app.
- **% within 25 cents:** how many of your notes landed close. Higher = tighter.

### About your tone (measured on your held notes)
- **Jitter / Shimmer:** think of your vocal cords as an idling engine.
  Jitter is tiny unevenness in *speed* (pitch); shimmer in *power*
  (loudness). Heard as roughness or breathiness — not as wrong notes. Lower
  is smoother. (These can be raised on purpose for gritty styles — that's
  fine, the system knows.)
- **HNR (dB):** clear tone vs breathy "static," like signal on a radio.
  Higher = cleaner. ~15+ is clean singing.
- **CPPS (dB):** a modern all-in-one clarity number. Higher = clearer.
- **Strain flags:** moments where you were high **and** loud **and** your
  tone lost clarity — the fingerprint of pushing. They come with timestamps
  so you can listen back. (Intentional grit sets them off too — trust your
  ears at the timestamp.)

### About your vibrato
- **Rate (Hz):** wobbles per second. Pros sit around **5–7**.
- **Extent (cents):** how wide the wobble is. Around 25–130 is healthy.
- **Onset delay (s):** pros often hold a note *straight* for a moment, then
  let vibrato bloom (about 0.2–0.6 s in). Landing straight-then-blooming
  reads as control; instant wobble can read as nerves.
- **Straight tone is allowed.** If you deliberately sing without vibrato,
  the system scores your *steadiness* instead and never nags you for
  "missing" vibrato.

### About how you enter and shape notes
- **Onsets (scoops / overshoots):** do you land clean on a note, slide *up*
  into it (scoop), or come in *above* and settle (overshoot)? None of these
  is wrong — they're **style**. What matters is being consistent. (For
  perspective: Al Green scoops more than half his notes. It's his sound.)
- **Registers / passaggio:** your chest voice vs your lighter head voice,
  and the "gear-change" point between them. Knowing where yours sits tells
  you which songs will feel like hard work.
- **Breath / phrase endings:** flagged spots where your pitch sags in the
  last half-second of a line — usually running out of air. Timestamped.
- **Dynamics:** your range from soft to loud, and whether your phrases have
  shape or sit at one volume.
- **Groove/timing:** how far ahead of or behind the beat you sing, in
  milliseconds. A little late can be soulful on purpose; wildly off is worth
  tightening.

### About your resonance (the "pro sound")
- **Singer's formant (dB):** the "ring" in the 2–4 kHz band that lets a
  voice cut *over* a band without shouting — like a headlight through fog.
  Higher (less negative) = more ring and projection.
- **H1−H2:** how "heavy" or "light" your voice production is — flutey and
  breathy at one end, pressed and rich at the other, balanced in the middle.

## 7. What the pictures mean

- **Traffic-light bar (top of the plot):** the song in 20-second chunks —
  green = clean, amber = keep an eye on it, red = trouble. Glance here first
  to see *where* to focus.
- **The blue line:** your sung pitch drawn over piano-key lanes. The shaded
  band is where your voice mostly lived; notes poking out of it are your
  reaches. (Turn on **Accuracy** to colour the line by how in-tune each
  moment was — off by default so you see the clean line first.)
- **Vibrato timeline:** orange marks = notes with vibrato (their speed);
  the green band is the pro 5–7 Hz zone; grey = straight-tone notes. You can
  see at a glance whether your vibrato sits in the pro range.
- **Vowel chart:** each dot is one held note, placed by its mouth-shape
  ("ee," "ah," "oo"…). Tight clusters mean consistent vowels across your
  range; scattered ones can mean your vowel changes as you go higher.
- **Spectrogram:** your voice split into its ingredients, stacked. The
  bottom bright line is the note itself; each stripe above is a harmonic
  (the overtones that make up your *tone*); the marked 2–4 kHz zone is where
  projection lives. On a **note card** (a close-up of one flagged note) a
  strained note shows its upper stripes going faint — that's what pushing
  *looks* like.

## 8. Comparing yourself to the original

If you gave the app a song + artist, it finds the real recording, measures
it the same way, and lines the two up:
- It **removes your key change** first, then tells you, section by section,
  whether you're **sharp or flat of the original melody** and **ahead of or
  behind** its phrasing.
- Scores are shown **capture-fair** on both sides (see Section 4) so the
  comparison is honest.
- Remember: singing something *differently* from the original can be
  deliberate artistry, not a mistake. The timestamps let you listen and
  decide.

---

## 9. Tracking your progress

Ask for your **progress ledger** and you'll get a table of every take of a
song with the key numbers, plus trend arrows ("intonation improving,
20→14 cents; drift improving 44→23"). This is where the work pays off
visibly. Compare raw numbers (cents, dB, %) across takes for the truest
picture.

## 10. Frequently asked

**"My score dropped — am I getting worse?"** Probably not. Early versions
of this tool *inflated* scores. The current one measures honestly against
professionals, so a real number can feel lower than a flattering one. Read
the components: if your intonation and tone are strong and only one thing is
low, you're in great shape with a clear target.

**"Why am I not beating the original when it sounds like I nailed it?"**
Because voice quality on an old/processed recording measures rough for
reasons that aren't the singer. Use the **capture-fair** comparison — and if
you still measure level with them on the things a singer controls (pitch,
vibrato, dynamics), you've genuinely earned it.

**"The app flagged a scoop / strain / sag but I did that on purpose."** Good
— trust yourself. Those flags are style-neutral prompts, not verdicts. The
timestamps are there so you can listen and decide what's intentional.

**"Which number should I care about most?"** The lowest component and its
prescribed drill. Fix the biggest lever first; re-record; watch it move.
