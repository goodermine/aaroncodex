## 2. DIAGNOSTIC TRIAGE

### Archetype Classification: **Volume Chaser / Hybrid (Pusher-adjacent)**

Aaron does not fit a single archetype cleanly. The dominant pattern is **Volume Chaser**: the instrument defaults to maximum output, with dynamic variation compressed into a narrow band. There is a secondary **Pusher** overlay — not in the sense of uncontrolled force, but in the structural reliance on upper-body muscular effort to generate and sustain the sound. This is not a technique fault so much as an architecture fault: the engine is in the wrong place.

### Mechanical vs Psychological Overlay

The mechanical layer is clear. The breath system is shallow, the support column is high (clavicular rather than abdominal), and the larynx compensates by bracing. The controlled grit is intentional and well-executed in terms of timbre, but it is being powered by a system that will fatigue.

The psychological overlay is revealing. This was an uninhibited performance — no competition pressure, no judges, no stakes beyond the desire to give everything. The result is a performer operating in full flow state: high situational awareness, seamless audience interaction, zero hesitation. The "freedom" context did not expose weakness; it exposed Aaron's natural performance ceiling. The tensions visible here are not nerves. They are structural defaults — the way the body has learned to produce this sound. That distinction matters enormously: this is not a fear problem. It is a plumbing problem.

### Physical Tension Map

The tension pattern follows a predictable gradient. The lower body is loose, rhythmic, and grounded — the hips and knees absorb the groove naturally. The upper body tells a different story. As vocal demand increases, the following cascade occurs:

This cascade is the physical manifestation of the breath architecture fault. The lower body knows how to be loose. The upper body has been recruited as the power source, and it braces accordingly.

This means the microphone is receiving a consistently high-SPL signal, and all dynamic control is happening at the source (the larynx). For a karaoke context this is standard, but it compounds the volume-chasing pattern: the system has no external pressure relief valve.

## 5. PRIMARY LIMITER

### The Single Highest-Leverage Coordination Fault

**The breath support column is anchored in the upper chest rather than the abdominal core, forcing the larynx and upper body to compensate for what the diaphragm and obliques should be managing.**

**What is happening biomechanically:** The inhale is clavicular — the chest rises, the shoulders lift, and the breath fills the upper lungs. On exhalation (phonation), there is no sustained elastic recoil from the abdominal wall to regulate subglottal pressure. Instead, the upper body braces — the torso locks, the shoulders rise further, and the neck muscles engage to stabilise the larynx against the unregulated air pressure from below. The larynx, now tasked with both pitch control and pressure management, rises and braces. The controlled grit is produced under these conditions, but the system is working at maximum structural load.

**Why it is happening:** This is a conditioned pattern, not a capability deficit. Aaron has the physical capacity for abdominal support — the loose, grounded lower body during rhythmic passages proves the musculature is available. The pattern likely developed because the controlled grit and high-volume output feel most powerful when the whole upper body is engaged. The sensation of effort in the neck and shoulders has become associated with "giving everything." The body has learned: effort = sound.

**What sensation replaces it:** The target sensation is **weight in the belt line**. On the inhale, expansion should be felt around the waistline and lower ribs — not the upper chest. During phonation, the sensation of effort should be a sustained, gentle outward push at the navel, as if bracing against a belt that is one notch too tight. The neck, shoulders, and jaw should feel as though they are doing nothing. The controlled grit should feel like it originates from the belly, not the throat. The emotional cue: **"Grit from the gut."**

## 6. TARGETED INTERVENTIONS (Top 2)

### Intervention 1: Messa di Voce (Dynamic Breath Control)

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Purpose** | Establish abdominal breath anchoring. Decouple volume from laryngeal bracing and shoulder elevation. Train the core to be the volume dial. |
| **How it should feel** | Expansion around the waistline on inhale. As volume increases on a sustained note, the effort sensation stays in the core — a gentle, sustained outward push below the navel. The neck, shoulders, and jaw remain completely passive. The crescendo feels like turning up a dial in the belly, not squeezing harder in the throat. |
| **Failure signal** | Shoulders rising on the inhale. Neck muscles tightening or jaw jutting forward as volume increases. The tone breaking, becoming breathy, or losing the controlled grit at higher volumes. Any sensation of "reaching" upward for volume. |
| **Success signal** | A smooth, continuous crescendo from pianissimo to fortissimo and back on a single pitch (start on B2 — Aaron's mean pitch) with zero visible movement in the upper chest or shoulders. The controlled grit can be layered in at the top of the crescendo without any change in the physical setup of the support column. |

### Intervention 2: Bratty Nay (Aryepiglottic Twang)

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Purpose** | Achieve the bright, cutting resonance required for the genre (high spectral centroid, forward placement) without relying on excessive subglottal pressure or laryngeal elevation. Train the resonance filter to do the work that raw volume is currently doing. |
| **How it should feel** | A highly concentrated, buzzy vibration behind the nose and in the hard palate. The sound should feel obnoxiously bright and "witchy" — almost cartoonish. The throat should feel open and relaxed. The sensation of loudness should come from the buzz in the face, not from effort in the chest or neck. |
| **Failure signal** | The sound becoming dark, swallowed, or throaty. Feeling a squeeze or scratchiness at the vocal fold level. Needing to push hard with the breath to make the sound loud. The "nay" losing its bratty, nasal edge. |
| **Success signal** | A piercing, penetrating tone that carries across a room with minimal breath pressure. The ability to sustain the "nay" on ascending scales (up to E4/F4) without any increase in physical effort or shoulder elevation. The sensation that the sound is "placed" in the face and requires almost no throat involvement. |

The grit is not being squeezed out of the throat. It is being pushed up from the floor, through the core, and projected forward through the face. The throat is just the corridor.
